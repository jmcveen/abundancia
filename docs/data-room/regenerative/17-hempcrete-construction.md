## 1. Executive Summary

Abundancia Austin will employ hempcrete (hemp-lime bio-composite) as its primary envelope material for all residential and community structures. This decision is driven by five converging factors: superior fire resistance in a wildfire-prone region, carbon-negative lifecycle performance, exceptional thermal and acoustic properties, proven multi-century durability, and alignment with the project's Living Building Challenge certification goals.

Hempcrete is not experimental. Over 10,000 hempcrete structures exist across Europe, with the oldest documented examples exceeding 600 years. France alone has permitted hempcrete construction since the 1990s, with over 5,000 completed buildings. Texas adopted hempcrete-compatible building codes, and the material's fire resistance directly addresses the catastrophic risk demonstrated by the 2011 Bastrop Complex Fire, which destroyed 1,673 homes within miles of our site.

Each Abundancia home will sequester an estimated 20-40 tons of CO2 within its walls, making the community's built environment a net carbon sink rather than an emissions source. At full buildout, the community's structures will have permanently removed an estimated 4,000-8,000 tons of CO2 from the atmosphere.

## 2. What Is Hempcrete

### 2.1 Definition

Hempcrete (also known as hemp-lime or hemp concrete) is a bio-composite building material composed of three primary ingredients:

- **Hemp hurds** (the woody inner core of the hemp stalk) — approximately 60-70% of the mix by volume
- **Lime-based binder** (hydraulic lime or a lime-pozzolan blend) — approximately 20-25% of the mix by volume
- **Water** — approximately 10-15% of the mix by volume

The resulting material is a lightweight, breathable, insulating infill that is cast around a structural frame (typically timber post-and-beam). Hempcrete is not load-bearing in the traditional sense; it provides the building envelope while the frame carries structural loads.

### 2.2 Historical Context

| Period | Development |
|--------|-------------|
| ~600 CE | Earliest known hemp-lime construction in Merovingian bridge abutments (France) |
| 1986 | Modern hempcrete developed by Charles Rasetti in France |
| 1990s | French building standards formalized for hemp-lime construction |
| 2000s | Spread to UK, Germany, Belgium, Canada, and Australia |
| 2009 | First permitted hempcrete building in the United States (Asheville, NC) |
| 2010s | US Hemp Building Association established; state-level codes adopted |
| 2018 | US Farm Bill legalizes industrial hemp cultivation nationwide |
| 2020s | Texas adopts hempcrete-compatible building codes; commercial hemp supply chain matures |
| 2024-25 | Multiple US hempcrete housing developments break ground |

### 2.3 The Science of Mineralization

Hempcrete's extraordinary durability arises from a process called carbonation. Over time, the lime binder absorbs atmospheric CO2 and converts back to calcium carbonate (limestone). This process:

- Continuously strengthens the material over decades and centuries
- Permanently locks sequestered carbon into the building envelope
- Creates an increasingly mineral matrix that resists biological degradation
- Explains the 500+ year lifespan observed in European structures

The chemical process: Ca(OH)2 + CO2 -> CaCO3 + H2O

This means hempcrete walls literally become stone over time, while the hemp hurds — fully encapsulated in the mineralized lime matrix — are preserved indefinitely.

## 6. Fire Performance

### 6.1 Fire Rating Details

Hempcrete achieves a 2+ hour fire resistance rating under ASTM E119 testing protocols. The mechanism of fire resistance involves three concurrent processes:

1. **Moisture release** — Hempcrete contains 10-15% equilibrium moisture that must be driven off before material temperatures rise, absorbing significant thermal energy
2. **Char layer formation** — Hemp hurds form a stable, insulating char layer that slows heat transfer through the wall
3. **Calcium carbonate endotherm** — The lime binder undergoes endothermic decomposition at ~840 degrees C, absorbing additional thermal energy
4. **Low thermal conductivity** — Even during fire exposure, the material's porosity limits heat transfer to the unexposed side

| Fire Test Parameter | Result |
|---------------------|--------|
| Time to flashover (unexposed side) | >120 minutes |
| Maximum temperature on unexposed side at 2 hours | <140 degrees C above ambient |
| Flame penetration | None at 2 hours |
| Structural integrity of frame | Maintained (char depth <15mm at 2 hours) |
| Smoke toxicity | Non-toxic — no HCN, minimal CO |
| Self-extinguishing | Yes — does not sustain combustion |

**Why homes were destroyed:** The vast majority of the 1,673 homes lost were conventional wood-frame construction. ## 7. Carbon Sequestration Analysis

### 7.1 Sequestration Mechanism

Carbon is sequestered in hempcrete through two distinct pathways:

1. **Biogenic carbon** — Hemp plants absorb CO2 during growth via photosynthesis. The carbon is permanently locked in the hemp hurds, which are encapsulated in the lime matrix and preserved indefinitely. Industrial hemp sequesters 1.5-1.8 tons of CO2 per ton of dry hemp hurds.

2. **Carbonation carbon** — The lime binder re-absorbs CO2 from the atmosphere as it cures and mineralizes. Over the first 50-100 years, the lime binder can re-absorb up to 75-80% of the CO2 released during its production (calcination).

## 10. Construction Process

### 10.1 Process Overview

| Phase | Duration | Activities |
|-------|----------|------------|
| 1. Foundation | 2-3 weeks | Slab-on-grade or pier-and-beam foundation |
| 2. Structural frame | 2-3 weeks | Timber post-and-beam erection |
| 3. Formwork installation | 1-2 weeks | Temporary forms set around frame |
| 4. Hempcrete mixing | Continuous during pour | On-site batch mixing (pan mixer or continuous) |
| 5. Hempcrete placement | 2-4 weeks | Pour in 18-24" lifts, light tamping |
| 6. Initial curing | 2-4 weeks | Forms stripped at 24-72 hours; air curing begins |
| 7. Continued curing | 4-8 weeks | Walls open to air; moisture content drops to <20% |
| 8. Lime render/plaster | 1-2 weeks | Exterior lime render + interior lime plaster |
| 9. Mechanical/electrical rough-in | 2-3 weeks | Systems installed (some pre-placed in formwork) |
| 10. Finish work | 3-5 weeks | Cabinetry, flooring, fixtures, final finishes |
| **Total construction timeline** | **16-30 weeks** | Comparable to conventional when crew is experienced |

### 10.2 Mixing Specifications

| Parameter | Specification |
|-----------|---------------|
| Mixer type | Pan mixer (preferred) or drum mixer with modifications |
| Batch size | 200-400 liters per batch |
| Mixing sequence | 1) Dry hemp + binder tumble 30 sec; 2) Add water gradually; 3) Mix 2-3 minutes |
| Consistency test | "Snowball test" — material holds shape when squeezed, breaks cleanly |
| Placement time after mixing | Within 30 minutes (before initial binder set) |
| Daily production rate (per crew) | 3-5 cubic meters |

### 10.3 Formwork System

| Component | Specification |
|-----------|---------------|
| Material | Marine plywood, reusable aluminum, or proprietary hempcrete forms |
| Wall thickness | 12-14 inches (standard wall) |
| Lift height | 18-24 inches per pour cycle |
| Tamping pressure | Light — 1-2 passes with flat tamper (not vibrated) |
| Form removal | 24-72 hours after pour (depending on ambient conditions) |
| Form reuse cycles | 20-50+ (plywood) or 200+ (aluminum) |

### 10.4 Curing Timeline

| Time After Pour | Condition | Activity |
|-----------------|-----------|----------|
| 0-24 hours | In formwork | Initial hydraulic set of binder |
| 24-72 hours | Form strip | Remove formwork; walls self-supporting |
| 1-4 weeks | Open curing | Fans/ventilation to accelerate drying |
| 4-8 weeks | Continued drying | Moisture content approaching 15-18% |
| 8-12 weeks | Ready for finishing | Moisture content <15%; lime render applied |
| 1-5 years | Ongoing carbonation | Strength continues to increase |
| 5-50+ years | Deep carbonation | Material becomes increasingly mineral/stone-like |

## 13. Warranty & Durability

### 13.1 Expected Lifespan

Hempcrete's extraordinary durability is not theoretical; it is documented across centuries of built examples:

| Structure | Location | Age | Condition |
|-----------|----------|-----|-----------|
| Merovingian bridge abutments | France | ~600 years | Intact — hemp fibers still identifiable in lime matrix |
| Medieval farmhouses (multiple) | France | 300-500 years | Occupied and functional |
| Japanese hemp-lime walls | Japan | 300+ years | Maintained and inhabited |
| La Maison de la Turquie | Nogent-sur-Seine, France | ~500 years | Restored and inhabited |
| Modern hempcrete buildings (1990s) | France | 30+ years | Excellent — no degradation |
| UK hempcrete homes (2000s) | Suffolk, UK | 20+ years | Monitoring shows improving performance |

### 13.2 Degradation Resistance

| Degradation Vector | Hempcrete Response | Conventional Wood Frame Response |
|--------------------|--------------------|----------------------------------|
| Moisture/rot | Lime matrix is alkaline (pH 12+); prevents all biological growth | Rot within months if moisture trapped |
| Termites/insects | Lime-encapsulated hurds are non-nutritive; alkaline environment repels | Active termite management required |
| Rodents | Dense, mineral material — not a food source or nesting substrate | Common entry/nesting material |
| UV degradation | Lime render surface — UV stable | Siding/paint degrades, requires replacement |
| Freeze-thaw cycling | Vapor-open assembly prevents moisture trapping; no spalling | Trapped moisture causes paint failure, wood damage |
| Seismic movement | Flexible, lightweight — accommodates movement | Rigid connections can crack and fail |

### 13.3 Warranty Structure

|-----------|----------------|----------|

| Hempcrete wall system | 25 years | Material integrity, thermal performance |
