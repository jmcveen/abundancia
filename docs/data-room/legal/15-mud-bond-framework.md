## 1. Executive Summary

The Abundancia Austin development intends to utilize a Municipal Utility District ("MUD") as a primary financing mechanism for major public infrastructure. MUD financing is a well-established tool in Texas real estate development that enables the issuance of tax-exempt bonds to fund water, wastewater, drainage, and road infrastructure. The developer advances the cost of infrastructure construction and is subsequently reimbursed through MUD bond proceeds, which are repaid by ad valorem property taxes levied within the district.

**Key Financial Impact:**

| Metric | Estimate |
|--------|----------|
| **Estimated Infrastructure Cost (MUD-Eligible)** | $8,000,000 - $12,000,000 |
| **Anticipated Bond Issuance** | Multiple series over 5-7 years |

| **Projected MUD Tax Rate** | $0.75 - $1.25 per $100 assessed value |

MUD financing is critical to the Abundancia Austin financial model. By shifting a substantial portion of infrastructure costs from upfront investor capital to long-term bond financing repaid by future property owners, the MUD structure significantly reduces the equity capital required from Limited Partners for horizontal development, improves project-level IRR, and accelerates the return of invested capital.

## 2. What is a Municipal Utility District

### 2.1 Definition

A Municipal Utility District is a special-purpose political subdivision of the State of Texas created to provide water, wastewater, drainage, and other utility-related services to properties within its boundaries. MUDs are governmental entities with the power to levy ad valorem taxes, issue bonds, and exercise eminent domain within their boundaries.

### 2.2 Historical Context

MUDs have been a cornerstone of Texas land development since the 1960s. Texas has more special districts than any other state, with over 1,200 active MUDs as of 2025. The MUD framework enabled the large-scale suburban and exurban development that characterizes the Houston, Dallas-Fort Worth, Austin, and San Antonio metropolitan areas. Virtually every major master-planned community in the greater Austin region has utilized MUD financing for infrastructure delivery.

### 2.3 How MUDs Work - Overview

1. **Creation.** The developer petitions the Texas Commission on Environmental Quality (TCEQ) to create a MUD encompassing the development property.
2. **Bond Authorization.** The MUD's board of directors (initially appointed by the developer, later elected by residents) authorizes the issuance of bonds to finance infrastructure.
3. **Developer Construction.** The developer constructs the infrastructure (water lines, sewer systems, drainage facilities, roads) using its own capital.
4. **Bond Issuance.** The MUD issues tax-exempt bonds and uses the proceeds to reimburse the developer for eligible infrastructure costs.
5. **Tax Levy.** The MUD levies an annual ad valorem property tax on all properties within the district to service the bond debt and fund ongoing operations and maintenance.
6. **Debt Retirement.** Over 20-30 years, property tax collections retire the bonds, and the MUD tax rate gradually declines as debt is paid off.

## 3. Texas MUD Law

### 3.1 Statutory Framework

MUDs in Texas are governed primarily by:

- **Texas Water Code, Chapter 49** - General provisions applicable to all water districts
- **Texas Water Code, Chapter 54** - Specific provisions for Municipal Utility Districts
- **Texas Commission on Environmental Quality (TCEQ) Rules** - 30 TAC Chapters 293 and 295
- **Texas Constitution, Article XVI, Section 59** - Authorization for conservation and reclamation districts

### 3.2 Powers of a MUD

Under Texas law, a MUD has the following powers:

| Power | Statutory Authority |
|-------|-------------------|
| Provide water and wastewater services | TWC 54.201 |
| Provide drainage and flood control | TWC 54.201 |
| Construct and maintain roads | TWC 54.234 (with voter approval) |
| Issue bonds | TWC 49.181 et seq. |
| Levy ad valorem taxes | TWC 49.107 |
| Exercise eminent domain | TWC 49.222 |
| Enter contracts | TWC 49.211 |
| Adopt and enforce rules | TWC 49.209 |
| Acquire, own, and operate facilities | TWC 54.201 |

### 3.3 TCEQ Oversight

TCEQ serves as the primary regulatory body for MUDs. TCEQ approval is required for:

- Creation of the MUD
- Issuance of bonds
- Engineering plans for infrastructure
- Water and wastewater treatment permits
- Financial reporting and audits

### 3.4 Bond Counsel and Legal Requirements

All MUD bonds must be approved by the Texas Attorney General prior to issuance. Bond counsel (a specialized municipal finance law firm) is engaged to render opinions on:

- Validity of the bonds
- Tax-exempt status of bond interest
- Compliance with all statutory requirements
- Enforceability of the bonds

## 4. MUD Creation Process

### 4.1 Pre-Petition Phase

Prior to filing a petition with TCEQ, the developer undertakes the following:

1. **Feasibility Study.** Engagement of a MUD financial advisor to model projected assessed values, tax rates, bond issuance capacity, and reimbursement timelines.
2. **Engineering Analysis.** Engagement of a MUD engineer to prepare preliminary infrastructure plans, cost estimates, and utility service plans.
3. **Legal Counsel.** Engagement of MUD counsel to prepare the creation petition, draft initial bylaws, and advise on TCEQ requirements.
4. **Boundary Determination.** Delineation of the MUD boundaries, typically coextensive with the development property (or a defined portion thereof).
5. **Consent Resolution.** If the property is within the ETJ (extraterritorial jurisdiction) of a municipality, the developer must obtain a consent resolution from the city council or enter into a strategic partnership agreement.

### 4.2 TCEQ Petition

The creation petition is filed with TCEQ and includes:

- Description of the proposed district boundaries (metes and bounds)
- Statement of the need for the district and the proposed services
- Preliminary engineering report and cost estimates
- Financial feasibility study
- Proof of landowner consent (100% consent if filed by the landowner/developer)
- Filing fee

### 4.3 TCEQ Review and Hearing

TCEQ conducts a review of the petition and holds a public hearing. The review process includes:

- Engineering review of proposed infrastructure
- Financial review of projected tax rates and bond capacity
- Environmental review (including endangered species considerations)
- Public notice and comment period
- TCEQ commissioners' hearing and vote

### 4.4 Confirmation Election

Following TCEQ approval, the MUD holds a confirmation election within its boundaries. At this election, voters (initially the developer or its agents, as the sole landowners within the district) confirm:

- Creation of the MUD
- Authorization to levy taxes
- Authorization to issue bonds (up to a specified amount)
- Election of the initial board of directors

### 4.5 Bastrop County Considerations

Bastrop County does not require separate county consent for MUD creation. The Abundancia Austin property is located outside any city's ETJ, which simplifies the creation process by eliminating the need for municipal consent. However, coordination with the Bastrop County Commissioners Court is advisable for road and drainage infrastructure that interfaces with county facilities.

## 5. Bond Issuance Mechanism

### 5.1 Types of MUD Bonds

| Bond Type | Purpose | Tax Treatment |
|-----------|---------|--------------|
| **Unlimited Tax Bonds (UTBs)** | Water, wastewater, drainage infrastructure | Tax-exempt interest |
| **Road Bonds** | Road construction within and serving the district | Tax-exempt interest |
| **Refunding Bonds** | Refinance existing bonds at lower rates | Tax-exempt interest |
| **Contract Revenue Bonds** | Revenue-backed facilities | Varies |

The Abundancia Austin MUD anticipates issuing primarily Unlimited Tax Bonds for water, wastewater, and drainage infrastructure, and Road Bonds for internal roadway construction.

### 5.2 Bond Issuance Process

1. **Developer Constructs Infrastructure.** The developer funds and constructs infrastructure per TCEQ-approved engineering plans.
2. **Engineer Certification.** The MUD engineer inspects completed infrastructure and certifies construction costs and compliance with approved plans.
3. **TCEQ Approval.** TCEQ reviews and approves the bond application, including:
   - Certified construction costs
   - Projected assessed values
   - Proposed tax rate
   - Debt service schedule
   - Financial advisor's feasibility report
4. **Attorney General Approval.** The Texas Attorney General reviews and approves the bonds.
5. **Bond Sale.** Bonds are sold in the public market (typically through negotiated sale with an underwriter).
6. **Developer Reimbursement.** Bond proceeds are used to reimburse the developer for eligible infrastructure costs, plus accrued interest on the developer's advance.

### 5.3 Bond Sizing and Capacity

Bond capacity is determined by the assessed value of property within the MUD and the projected tax rate. Key factors:

- **Assessed Value.** Based on appraisal of land, improvements, and personal property within the district.
- **Tax Rate.** The combined MUD tax rate (debt service + operations & maintenance) must remain within market-acceptable levels (typically $0.75 - $1.50 per $100 AV for competitive developments).
- **Debt Service Coverage.** Bond underwriters require a minimum debt service coverage ratio (DSCR), typically 1.25x - 1.50x.
- **Market Conditions.** Interest rates and investor appetite for municipal bonds affect pricing and timing.

## 6. Infrastructure Funded by MUD

### 6.1 Eligible Infrastructure Categories

The following infrastructure categories are eligible for MUD bond reimbursement:

**Water Distribution System**
- Water transmission lines (12" and larger)
- Water distribution lines (6" - 12")
- Water storage facilities (elevated and ground storage tanks)
- Booster pump stations
- Water well facilities (if applicable)
- Connections to regional water supply (e.g., Aqua Water Supply Corporation or other provider)
- Fire hydrants and fire flow infrastructure
- Water meters and service connections

**Wastewater Collection System**
- Sanitary sewer collection lines (gravity and force main)
- Lift stations and pump stations
- Wastewater treatment facility or connection fees to regional treatment plant
- Manholes, cleanouts, and appurtenances
- Service laterals to lot lines

**Drainage and Stormwater Management**
- Storm sewer lines and culverts
- Detention and retention ponds
- Open channel drainage improvements
- Erosion control measures
- Low-impact development (LID) features (bioswales, rain gardens) if qualifying
- Regional drainage facilities serving the district

**Roads and Transportation**
- Internal collector and local streets
- Paving, curbing, and sidewalks
- Street lighting
- Traffic signage and signals
- Bridge and culvert crossings
- Road connection to FM 969, SH 71, or other county/state facilities

**Shared and Ancillary Facilities**
- Engineering, surveying, and geotechnical costs
- Construction management and inspection fees
- Regulatory permit and filing fees
- Interest on developer advances (eligible for reimbursement)
- Legal, financial advisory, and bond counsel fees

### 6.2 Ineligible Costs

The following costs are generally not eligible for MUD bond reimbursement:

- Land acquisition costs
- Private lot improvements (homes, buildings)
- Amenity facilities (clubhouse, pool, parks) unless qualifying as public facilities
- Marketing and sales expenses
- Developer overhead and profit margin on construction
- Landscaping beyond public rights-of-way

## 8. Developer Reimbursement Structure

### 8.1 How Reimbursement Works

The developer funds infrastructure construction with its own capital (drawn from LP investments in Series B - Infrastructure). Upon completion of each infrastructure component, the MUD engineer certifies the costs, TCEQ approves the bond issuance, and the MUD sells bonds. Bond proceeds are then paid to the developer as reimbursement.

### 8.2 Reimbursement Components

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Hard Costs** | Actual construction costs per certified engineer's report |
| **Soft Costs** | Engineering, surveying, geotechnical, environmental, permitting |
| **Interest on Advances** | Interest accrued on the developer's capital from the date of expenditure to the date of reimbursement, at the rate specified in the developer's reimbursement agreement with the MUD (typically prime + 1-2%) |
| **Bond Issuance Costs** | Underwriting discount, bond counsel fees, financial advisor fees, printing, rating agency fees |

### 8.3 Reimbursement Agreement

The developer and the MUD enter into a formal Reimbursement Agreement that specifies:

- Categories of eligible infrastructure costs
- Interest rate on developer advances
- Priority of reimbursement
- Maximum reimbursement amount
- Conditions precedent to bond issuance
- Representations and warranties

The Reimbursement Agreement is a binding contract between the developer and the MUD, approved by the MUD's board of directors and its bond counsel.

### 8.4 Reimbursement Timeline

| Milestone | Estimated Timeline |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Developer begins infrastructure construction | Month 0 |
| Construction complete, engineer certifies costs | Month 12-18 |
| TCEQ bond application filed | Month 14-20 |
| TCEQ approval received | Month 18-24 |
| Attorney General approval | Month 19-25 |
| Bond sale and closing | Month 20-26 |
| Developer receives reimbursement | Month 20-26 |

**Typical gap between expenditure and reimbursement: 18-30 months.** This gap is a key consideration in the project's capital planning and cash flow management. Interest accrual on developer advances partially compensates for the time value of money.

### 8.5 Impact on Investor Returns

MUD reimbursement is a significant capital event for the project and its investors:

- **Capital Recycling.** Reimbursement returns infrastructure capital to the series, which can be distributed to LPs or redeployed into subsequent phases.
- **IRR Enhancement.** By recovering 80-90% of infrastructure costs through bond proceeds (plus accrued interest), the effective equity investment in infrastructure is reduced, improving project-level IRR.
- **Preferred Return Acceleration.** MUD reimbursements contribute to the cash available for LP preferred return and return of capital distributions.

## 10. Benefits to the Project

### 10.1 Reduced Upfront Capital Requirement

Without MUD financing, the entire cost of public infrastructure (estimated at $8M - $12M) would need to be funded from investor equity. MUD bonds shift this burden to long-term tax-supported debt, reducing the equity capital required from LPs by approximately 60-70% for infrastructure costs.

### 10.2 Capital Stack Optimization

| Scenario | Infrastructure Equity Required | Total Equity Required ($12.5M raise) | Infrastructure as % of Total |
|----------|-------------------------------|--------------------------------------|------------------------------|
| **Without MUD** | $8M - $12M | $12.5M | 64% - 96% |
| **With MUD** | $2M - $4M (bridge funding until reimbursement) | $12.5M | 16% - 32% |

MUD financing frees a substantial portion of investor equity for land acquisition, vertical construction, amenity development, and operating reserves --- uses with higher return potential than infrastructure.

### 10.3 Competitive Home Pricing

MUD bond financing is standard in Central Texas and is already priced into homebuyer expectations. Bastrop County's low base tax rate (no city taxes for unincorporated areas) means that even with a MUD overlay, the total tax burden remains competitive.

### 10.4 Infrastructure Quality

MUD-financed infrastructure must meet TCEQ engineering standards, ensuring high-quality water, wastewater, and drainage systems. This reduces long-term maintenance costs and provides a reliable utility platform for the community.

### 10.5 Self-Governing Utility District

The MUD provides a dedicated governance structure for utility operations, with an elected board of directors accountable to district residents. This ensures ongoing professional management of water, wastewater, and drainage services.

## 12. Risk Factors

### 12.1 MUD Creation Risk

**Risk:** TCEQ may deny or delay the MUD creation petition due to regulatory, environmental, or procedural issues.

**Mitigation:** Engagement of experienced MUD counsel and engineer with track records of successful MUD creations in Central Texas. Pre-petition coordination with TCEQ staff. Comprehensive environmental review (including Houston toad compliance under LPHCP) completed prior to filing.

### 12.2 Bond Market Risk

**Risk:** Rising interest rates or unfavorable municipal bond market conditions may increase borrowing costs or delay bond issuance.

**Mitigation:** Phased bond issuance strategy allows flexibility in timing. Conservative financial projections account for rate variability. The MUD financial advisor monitors market conditions to optimize issuance timing.

### 12.3 Assessed Value Risk

**Risk:** Assessed property values within the MUD may be lower than projected, reducing bond capacity or requiring higher tax rates.

**Mitigation:** Conservative underwriting assumptions in the financial model. Development pace aligned with market absorption to support assessed value growth. Phased bond issuance tied to actual (not projected) assessed values.

### 12.4 Reimbursement Timing Risk

**Risk:** Delays in TCEQ approval or bond market conditions could extend the gap between developer expenditure and reimbursement beyond projected timelines.

**Mitigation:** Adequate capital reserves in Series B to carry infrastructure costs for an extended period. Interest accrual on developer advances provides partial compensation. Conservative cash flow projections assume a 24-30 month reimbursement lag.

### 12.5 Tax Rate Competitiveness Risk

**Risk:** If the combined MUD and overlapping tax rate exceeds market norms, home prices and absorption rates may be adversely affected.

**Mitigation:** Total projected tax rate of $2.42 per $100 AV is at the low end of comparable developments. The absence of city taxes (unincorporated Bastrop County) provides significant rate headroom. Active tax rate management through phased bond issuance and refinancing.

### 12.6 Regulatory Change Risk

**Risk:** Changes to Texas MUD law, TCEQ rules, or federal tax treatment of municipal bonds could affect the MUD structure or bond economics.

**Mitigation:** Texas MUD law has been stable for decades and enjoys strong bipartisan political support. The state's development model is heavily dependent on MUDs, making significant adverse changes unlikely. Bond counsel monitors legislative developments.

### 12.7 Environmental Compliance Risk

**Risk:** Houston toad (Bufo houstonensis) habitat considerations in Bastrop County may affect MUD infrastructure design, routing, and costs.

**Mitigation:** Compliance with the Lost Pines Habitat Conservation Plan (LPHCP) integrated into infrastructure design from inception. Environmental consultants engaged during pre-petition phase. Infrastructure corridors designed to minimize habitat disturbance. See Document 16 for comprehensive environmental risk analysis.

## 14. Glossary of Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Ad Valorem Tax** | A tax based on the assessed value of property |

| **O&M Rate** | Operations and Maintenance tax rate; the portion of the MUD tax used for district operations |

| **TCEQ** | Texas Commission on Environmental Quality |
| **TPDES** | Texas Pollutant Discharge Elimination System |
| **TWC** | Texas Water Code |
