## 1. Executive Summary

### 1.1 Purpose

This Blue Sky Memorandum has been prepared for Abundancia Austin LLC (the "Company") in connection with its offering (the "Offering") of membership interest units (the "Units") pursuant to Regulation D, Rule 506(c) under the Securities Act of 1933, as amended (the "Securities Act"). The purpose of this memorandum is to summarize the federal and state securities law requirements applicable to the Offering, including registration exemptions, notice filing obligations, filing fees, timing requirements, and ongoing compliance obligations.

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Issuer | Abundancia Austin LLC (Texas LLC) |
| Managing Member | Light Brands Consulting LLC |
| Manager | Nicholas Lawless |
| Securities Offered | Membership Interest Units |
| Maximum Units | 250 |
| Price Per Unit | $50,000.00 |
| Maximum Offering Amount | $12,500,000.00 |

| Project | Regenerative luxury community, 120 acres, Bastrop County, Texas |

### 1.3 Key Findings

1. **Federal Preemption.** Rule 506(c) offerings are "covered securities" under Section 18 of the Securities Act (as added by NSMIA), which preempts state registration and qualification requirements. States may not impose substantive merit review or registration requirements on the Offering.

2. **State Notice Filings Required.** Although substantive state regulation is preempted, most states require notice filings (typically Form D or a uniform state filing) and payment of filing fees. Failure to make timely notice filings may result in penalties but does not affect the validity of the federal exemption.

3. **Accredited Investor Verification.** Rule 506(c) requires the issuer to take "reasonable steps" to verify the accredited investor status of each purchaser. This is a heightened standard compared to Rule 506(b), which only requires a reasonable belief. Verification methods are prescribed by Rule 506(c)(2)(ii).

4. **Form D Filing.** A Form D must be filed with the SEC no later than 15 calendar days after the first sale of securities. Amendments must be filed for material changes and annually if the offering is continuing.

5. **Estimated Compliance Budget.** Total first-year blue sky compliance costs are estimated at $21,950 to $25,450 for all-states filing, with annual ongoing costs of approximately $18,950 to $20,450.

## 2. Federal Securities Law Framework

### 2.1 Securities Act of 1933

The Securities Act requires that all offers and sales of securities be registered with the SEC unless an exemption from registration is available. Section 5 of the Securities Act prohibits:

- Offering securities for sale without a registration statement being filed (Section 5(c))
- Selling securities unless a registration statement is in effect (Section 5(a))
- Delivering securities without a prospectus meeting Section 10 requirements (Section 5(b))

### 2.2 Regulation D Overview

Regulation D (17 C.F.R. Sections 230.501-508) provides a set of safe harbor exemptions from Section 5 registration requirements. The three principal exemptions under Regulation D are:

| Rule | Maximum Raise | Investor Limitations | General Solicitation | Verification |
|------|---------------|---------------------|---------------------|--------------|
| Rule 504 | $10M (12 months) | No limit (varies by state) | Generally not permitted | Not required |
| Rule 506(b) | Unlimited | Up to 35 non-accredited investors | **Not permitted** | Reasonable belief |
| Rule 506(c) | Unlimited | **Accredited investors only** | **Permitted** | **Reasonable steps to verify** |

### 2.3 Rule 506(c) -- The Applicable Exemption

The Company is conducting the Offering pursuant to Rule 506(c), which was adopted by the SEC in 2013 pursuant to the Jumpstart Our Business Startups Act (the "JOBS Act"). Rule 506(c) provides that:

1. The issuer may use general solicitation and general advertising to market the offering
2. All purchasers must be accredited investors as defined in Rule 501(a)
3. The issuer must take **reasonable steps to verify** that each purchaser is an accredited investor
4. The issuer must satisfy the other conditions of Regulation D (including the filing of Form D)
5. No disqualifying events under Rule 506(d) exist with respect to any covered person

### 2.4 Accredited Investor Definition

Under Rule 501(a), as amended effective March 2021, accredited investors include the following categories:

**Natural Persons:**

| Category | Qualification |
|----------|---------------|
| Income Test | Individual income exceeding $200,000 in each of the two most recent years (or joint income with spouse/spousal equivalent exceeding $300,000), with a reasonable expectation of reaching the same income level in the current year |
| Net Worth Test | Individual net worth, or joint net worth with spouse/spousal equivalent, exceeding $1,000,000, excluding the value of the primary residence |
| Professional Certifications | Holders of Series 7, Series 65, or Series 82 licenses in good standing |
| Knowledgeable Employees | Of a private fund, as defined in Rule 3c-5(a)(4) of the Investment Company Act |

**Entity Investors:**

| Category | Qualification |
|----------|---------------|
| Banks and Insurance Companies | As defined in Section 3(a)(2) and Section 2(a)(13) of the Securities Act |
| Registered Investment Companies | Under the Investment Company Act of 1940 |
| Business Development Companies | As defined in Section 2(a)(48) of the Investment Company Act |
| 501(c)(3) Organizations | With total assets exceeding $5,000,000 |
| Trusts | With total assets exceeding $5,000,000 and a sophisticated person directing investments |
| Entities Owned by Accredited Investors | All equity owners are accredited investors |
| Any Entity | With investments exceeding $5,000,000 (not formed for the specific purpose of acquiring the securities) |
| Family Offices | With at least $5,000,000 in assets under management and their family clients |
| RIAs and ERISAs | SEC- or state-registered investment advisers; Rural Business Investment Companies |

### 2.5 Verification Methods Under Rule 506(c)

Rule 506(c)(2)(ii) provides the following non-exclusive safe harbor verification methods:

**For Income-Based Accreditation:**
- Reviewing IRS forms (W-2, K-1, tax returns, 1099) for the two most recent years, plus obtaining a written representation regarding reasonable expectation for the current year

**For Net Worth-Based Accreditation:**
- Reviewing bank statements, brokerage statements, certificates of deposit, tax assessments, and third-party appraisal reports (dated within three months), and checking a consumer report for outstanding liabilities

**Third-Party Verification:**
- Obtaining written confirmation from a registered broker-dealer, SEC-registered investment adviser, licensed attorney, or CPA that has taken reasonable steps to verify accredited status within the prior three months

**Existing Investor Certification:**
- For investors who previously invested as accredited investors and remain invested, obtaining a certification of continued accredited status at the time of the new investment

**Principles-Based Analysis:**
- Where no safe harbor method is used, the SEC considers the totality of circumstances, including: the nature of the purchaser, the amount of the investment, the type of information available, and the nature of the solicitation

## 3. Federal Preemption Under Rule 506(c)

### 3.1 National Securities Markets Improvement Act of 1996 (NSMIA)

The National Securities Markets Improvement Act of 1996 ("NSMIA") added Section 18 to the Securities Act, which designates securities offered or sold pursuant to Rule 506 as "covered securities." Section 18(a) provides:

> *"No law, rule, regulation, or order, or other administrative action of any State ... requiring, or with respect to, registration or qualification of securities ... shall directly or indirectly apply to a security that is a covered security."*

### 3.2 Scope of Preemption

The federal preemption under NSMIA applies to Rule 506(c) offerings as follows:

| Preempted (States Cannot) | Preserved (States May) |
|---------------------------|------------------------|
| Require registration or qualification of the securities | Require notice filings and collect filing fees |
| Impose merit review or substantive conditions on the offering | Investigate and bring enforcement actions for fraud |
| Require specific disclosure documents beyond federal requirements | Require filing of Form D or equivalent notice |
| Limit the number or type of investors | Require consent-to-service-of-process filings |
| Restrict general solicitation beyond federal standards | Suspend the notice filing exemption for failure to file |
| Require pre-approval of advertising materials | Request copies of materials for anti-fraud review |

### 3.3 Significance for the Offering

Because the Offering is conducted under Rule 506(c), the Company's securities are "covered securities" and the Offering is preempted from state registration requirements in all 50 states, the District of Columbia, and U.S. territories. This means:

1. The Company does not need to register the Units with any state securities regulator
2. No state can require merit review of the Offering or the PPM
3. No state can impose conditions on the terms of the Offering beyond what federal law requires
4. The Company's obligation at the state level is limited to **notice filings and fees**
5. States retain jurisdiction over **anti-fraud enforcement**

### 3.4 JOBS Act Confirmation

The JOBS Act of 2012 directed the SEC to adopt rules permitting general solicitation in Rule 506 offerings (resulting in Rule 506(c)). The SEC confirmed in Release No. 33-9415 (July 2013) that NSMIA preemption applies equally to Rule 506(c) offerings that use general solicitation. Several states initially challenged whether preemption applied to offerings using general solicitation, but the SEC and courts have consistently affirmed that NSMIA preemption extends to Rule 506(c) offerings without limitation.

### 3.5 Dodd-Frank Act Considerations

The Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act of 2010 did not alter the NSMIA preemption framework for Rule 506 offerings. However, Section 926 of Dodd-Frank directed the SEC to adopt "bad actor" disqualification rules (now Rule 506(d)), which the Company has addressed in Document 24 (Bad Actor Certification) of the Data Room.

## 4. Form D Filing Requirements

### 4.1 SEC Form D

Form D is the notice of an exempt offering of securities filed with the SEC pursuant to Regulation D. Form D is not a registration statement and does not subject the Offering to SEC review or approval.

### 4.2 Filing Deadline

| Filing Type | Deadline | Notes |
|-------------|----------|-------|
| Initial Filing | 15 calendar days after the first sale of securities | "First sale" means receipt of a binding subscription and investment commitment |
| Annual Amendment | Within 30 days after the anniversary of the initial filing, if the offering is still continuing | Required even if no additional sales have occurred |
| Material Change Amendment | Promptly after any material change to the information in Form D | No specific deadline, but should be filed as soon as practicable |
| Final Amendment | Upon completion of the Offering | To indicate the offering has been completed |

### 4.3 Form D Content

Form D requires disclosure of the following information:

| Item | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Item 1 | Issuer identity (Abundancia Austin LLC, Texas, Bastrop County) |
| Item 2 | Principal place of business and telephone |
| Item 3 | Related persons (Nicholas Lawless, Manager; Light Brands Consulting LLC, Managing Member) |
| Item 4 | Industry group (Real Estate) |
| Item 5 | Issuer size (revenue range or aggregate net asset value) |
| Item 6 | Federal exemption claimed (Rule 506(c)) |
| Item 7 | Type of filing (new notice, amendment) |
| Item 8 | Duration of offering |
| Item 9 | Type(s) of securities offered (membership interests / limited liability company units) |
| Item 10 | Business combination transaction (N/A) |
| Item 11 | Minimum investment accepted ($50,000) |
| Item 12 | Sales compensation (broker-dealers, finders fees, if any) |
| Item 13 | Offering and sales amounts ($12,500,000 total; amount sold; amount remaining) |
| Item 14 | Investors (number who have already invested, number who are accredited) |
| Item 15 | Sales commissions and finders' fees |
| Item 16 | Use of proceeds |

### 4.4 Electronic Filing

Form D must be filed electronically through the SEC's Electronic Data Gathering, Analysis, and Retrieval system (EDGAR). The Company must first obtain EDGAR access codes by filing Form ID. The process typically takes 2-3 weeks.

### 4.5 Consequences of Late Filing

Failure to file Form D does not invalidate the Rule 506(c) exemption. However:

1. The SEC has taken the position that timely Form D filing is a condition of Rule 507, which provides a disqualification from Regulation D for issuers that have been enjoined for failure to file Form D
2. Late filing may trigger state enforcement actions
3. Late filing may raise concerns in subsequent offerings or regulatory examinations
4. Best practice is to file Form D promptly and within the 15-day deadline
5. The SEC proposed (but has not adopted) amendments that would condition the Rule 506 exemption on timely Form D filing

## 5. State Notice Filing Requirements

### 5.1 Overview

Although NSMIA preempts state registration requirements for Rule 506(c) offerings, Section 18(c)(2) of the Securities Act preserves the states' authority to:

1. Require the filing of any document filed with the SEC (i.e., Form D)
2. Require the payment of filing fees
3. Require a consent to service of process (Form U-2)

### 5.2 Uniform Consent to Service of Process (Form U-2)

Most states require the filing of a Uniform Consent to Service of Process (Form U-2 or equivalent) in connection with the notice filing. This form designates the state securities administrator as agent for service of process for any enforcement action. Filing Form U-2:

- Authorizes the state administrator to accept service on behalf of the Company
- Does not constitute general consent to jurisdiction
- Is a ministerial requirement that does not subject the Offering to merit review
- Must typically be filed concurrently with the state notice filing

### 5.3 When State Notice Filings Are Required

State notice filings are required in each state where the Company sells securities to investors. The filing obligation is triggered by the sale to an investor in that state, not by solicitation or offering activity.

### 5.4 Filing Deadlines by State Category

States generally fall into three categories with respect to filing deadlines:

| Category | Timing | States |
|----------|--------|--------|
| **Pre-Sale Filing** | Before the first sale to a resident of the state | New York, Wisconsin |
| **Concurrent Filing** | At or before the time of the first sale | Connecticut, Maryland, Tennessee, Washington |
| **Post-Sale Filing** | Within 15 days after the first sale (or within 15 days after SEC Form D filing) | Majority of states, including Texas, California, Florida |

### 5.5 Electronic Filing Systems

Most states accept electronic notice filings through one or both of the following systems:

| System | Description | Coverage |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| **NASAA Electronic Filing Depository (EFD)** | Centralized electronic filing system operated by the North American Securities Administrators Association | 48 states + DC accept EFD filings |
| **Direct State Filing** | State-specific filing portals or paper submissions | New York, Florida (also accept EFD) |

## 6. Key State Considerations -- Texas

### 6.1 Texas Securities Act

The Company is organized in Texas and the Project is located in Bastrop County, Texas. Texas securities law is governed by The Securities Act (Tex. Gov't Code Ch. 4001-4008, effective January 1, 2022, formerly Tex. Rev. Civ. Stat. Ann. art. 581) and the rules adopted by the Texas State Securities Board.

### 6.2 Notice Filing Requirements

| Requirement | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| Filing Authority | Texas State Securities Board |
| Filing Deadline | Not later than 15 days after the first sale to a Texas resident |
| Documents Required | Copy of Form D as filed with the SEC; Form 133.28 (Notice Filing for Covered Securities) |
| Consent to Service of Process | Form U-2 |
| Filing Fee | $300.00 |
| Renewal | Annual renewal required with fee of $300.00 if the offering continues beyond one year |
| Filing Method | Electronic via EFD or direct submission |

### 6.3 Texas-Specific Considerations

1. **Home State Jurisdiction.** As the Company's state of organization and the state where the Project is located, Texas regulators may take particular interest in the Offering. All disclosures should be accurate and complete with respect to the Project's Bastrop County location, 120 acres, permits, and regulatory compliance.

2. **Texas State Securities Board Enforcement.** The Board has broad enforcement authority including cease-and-desist orders, administrative fines up to $10,000 per violation, rescission orders, and criminal referrals. While the Board cannot require registration of covered securities, it retains full authority over fraud enforcement.

3. **Real Estate Considerations.** The Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC) generally does not regulate the sale of securities (as opposed to the sale of real estate), but the Company should ensure that the Offering structure clearly qualifies as a securities offering rather than a sale of real estate interests that might trigger additional licensing requirements. The membership interest structure of the Company supports securities treatment.

4. **Anti-Fraud Standards.** Texas Gov't Code Section 4007.101 prohibits fraud in the offer or sale of securities, including material misstatements and omissions. This standard applies regardless of whether the securities are registered or exempt.

5. **Bastrop County Local Requirements.** The Company should confirm that no additional local filings or disclosures are required in connection with the sale of securities relating to a Bastrop County real estate project. While unusual, some municipalities have adopted local consumer protection ordinances.

### 6.4 Texas Filing Process

1. File Form D with the SEC electronically via EDGAR
2. File a copy of Form D with the Texas State Securities Board via EFD or direct submission
3. File Form 133.28 (Notice Filing for Covered Securities)
4. File Form U-2 (Consent to Service of Process)
5. Submit filing fee of $300.00
6. Retain copies of all filed documents

The Company should consult with tax counsel regarding California tax nexus, including the $800 minimum franchise tax.

5. **Renewal Filing.** California requires an annual renewal filing with applicable fees if the offering continues beyond the initial filing period.

6. **DFPI Inquiry Authority.** The DFPI may request copies of offering documents and investor communications for anti-fraud review purposes. The Company should be prepared to provide these promptly upon request.

### 8.4 New York-Specific Considerations

Unlike federal securities fraud claims, the Martin Act does not require proof of scienter (intent to defraud) or reliance. A showing of material misrepresentation or omission is sufficient.

3. **Real Estate Syndication Regulations.** The New York Attorney General's Office has historically scrutinized real estate syndications. While NSMIA preemption limits substantive state regulation, the Company should be aware that the Attorney General may take a heightened interest in real estate offerings sold to New York residents.

5. **Form 99 Requirements.** Form 99 requires additional issuer information beyond what is contained in Form D, including details about the principals, prior offerings, and use of proceeds.

6. **Renewal.** Annual renewal filing is required if the offering continues beyond one year, with payment of applicable fees.

## 14. Anti-Fraud Provisions

### 14.1 Federal Anti-Fraud Rules

The following federal anti-fraud provisions apply to the Offering regardless of the exemption from registration:

| Provision | Citation | Standard |
|-----------|----------|----------|

5. Maintain a record of all communications with investors
6. Do not guarantee returns or make promises of specific investment outcomes
7. Ensure all persons involved in the Offering understand their anti-fraud obligations

## 15. Penalties for Non-Compliance

### 15.1 Federal Penalties

| Violation | Potential Penalty |

| Failure to file Form D | Potential Rule 507 disqualification from future Regulation D offerings |

| Category | Potential Penalty |

## 16. Broker-Dealer and Finder Considerations

### 16.1 Broker-Dealer Registration

Any person or entity that receives transaction-based compensation (commissions, success fees, or similar payments) in connection with the Offering may be required to register as a broker-dealer under Section 15 of the Exchange Act and applicable state laws.

### 16.2 Impact on Blue Sky Compliance

If the Company engages a registered broker-dealer to assist with the Offering:

1. The broker-dealer's CRD number must be disclosed on Form D (Item 12)
2. Sales compensation must be disclosed on Form D
3. The broker-dealer must comply with its own state registration and notice requirements
4. The broker-dealer is subject to FINRA rules regarding private placement due diligence (FINRA Rule 5122/5123)
5. The broker-dealer may need to file selling agreements in certain states

### 16.3 Finder Arrangements

The SEC has proposed (but not adopted) a limited exemption for finders in private placements. Under current law:

1. **Tier 1 Finders** (single transaction, no solicitation) may not require registration in certain circumstances
2. **Tier 2 Finders** (multiple transactions, limited solicitation) present greater regulatory risk
3. The Company should consult with securities counsel before engaging any finder
4. All finder arrangements must be disclosed on Form D
5. State-level finder regulations vary significantly and must be analyzed on a state-by-state basis

### 16.4 State Broker-Dealer Considerations

Several states have additional requirements for persons participating in the sale of securities:

| State | Requirement |

## 18. General Solicitation Compliance

### 18.1 Rule 506(c) General Solicitation

Rule 506(c) permits the use of general solicitation and general advertising in connection with the offering, provided that:

- All purchasers are accredited investors
- The issuer takes reasonable steps to verify each purchaser's accredited investor status
- The offering otherwise satisfies the conditions of Rule 506
- No disqualifying events exist under Rule 506(d)

5. Records of all solicitation materials and their distribution should be maintained for a minimum of 5 years
6. Materials should clearly state that the offering is limited to accredited investors
7. The Company should maintain a log of all general solicitation activities by date, channel, and audience

## 20. Recommendations

### 20.1 Priority Recommendations

Based on the analysis in this memorandum, we recommend the following:

1. **Engage a qualified filing service or securities counsel** to handle all Form D and state notice filings. The cost of professional filing services ($3,000-$5,000) is modest relative to the potential penalties for non-compliance.

2. **File in all 50 states proactively.** For a total estimated cost of approximately $17,450, all-states filing provides maximum flexibility to accept investors from any state without the risk of missing a filing deadline. This is particularly important given that the Company will use general solicitation under Rule 506(c), which may reach investors in all states.

3. **Prioritize New York and Wisconsin pre-sale filings.** Given New York's pre-sale filing requirement and the Martin Act's broad enforcement provisions, the Company should file in New York before commencing any solicitation activities that may reach New York residents. Wisconsin also requires pre-sale filing.

4. **Implement a centralized compliance tracking system** for state filing deadlines, renewal dates, and amendment requirements. A compliance calendar should be maintained by the Company's compliance officer or securities counsel.

5. **Maintain comprehensive records** of all general solicitation activities, accredited investor verifications, state filings, and investor communications. These records are essential for demonstrating compliance in the event of a regulatory inquiry.

6. **Review all marketing materials** with securities counsel before distribution to ensure compliance with anti-fraud provisions and Rule 506(c) general solicitation standards.

7. **Monitor regulatory developments.** The SEC and state securities regulators periodically update rules, fees, and filing procedures. The Company should subscribe to regulatory updates from NASAA and the SEC.

8. **Conduct quarterly compliance reviews** to verify that all filings are current, all investors have been properly verified, and all records are complete.
